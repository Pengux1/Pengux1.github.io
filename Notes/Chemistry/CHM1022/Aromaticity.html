Benzene's Structure and Representations

Benzene is an important organic chemical compound with the chemical formula C₆H₆. The benzene molecule is composed of six carbon atoms joined in a ring with one hydrogen atom attached to each. As it contains only carbon and hydrogen atoms, benzene is classed as a hydrocarbon. Its structure can be seen to the left.

Benzene's Unique Stability

Benzene has 6 sp2 hybridised carbon atoms forming a perfect hexagon with C-C-C and C-C-H bond angles of 120°. This shape allows carbon atoms to share electrons, making them delocalised. As a general rule, the more spread out or delocalised electrons are in a molecule, the more stable it becomes. By sharing electrons, all the carbon atoms in a benzene ring get 6 stable double bonds and 8 electrons in their outer shell.

The six π bonds are delocalised and can move freely about the six carbon nuclei (π cloud), stabilising the bezene molecule.

Aromaticity

Aromatic compounds are a class of molecules with special characteristics associated with the planar ring systems with delocalised π electrons. Aromatic molecules do not always look the same, but have a set of characteristics that they must meet to qualify as being aromatic. These are:

  1. They must be cyclic.
  2. Electrons must be conjugated all arounf the ring (Double bonds alternate with single bonds, and the electrons are delocalised)
  3. They must be planar
  4. They must obey Huckel's Rule, which states that the molecule must have (4n+2) π electrons, where n is an integer.

Huckle's Rule

Any compound with a planar ring system and a delocalized π-system containing (4n+2) π-electrons, where n is an integer, including neutral molecules, anions and cations, is AROMATIC.

Mono-Substituted Benzene Rings

The mono-substituted benzene derivative containing a CH3 group attached to the ring is known as toluene. Its IUPAC systematic name is methylbenzene. Some aromatic molecules have acquired non-systematic names (i.e. hydroxybenzene is phenol, aminobenzene is aniline).

Di-Substituted Benzene Rings

For a benzene ring with two subsitutents, we use ortho, meta and para to denote relative positions of the substituents, as can be seen in the image.

Poly-Substituted Benzene Rings

For polysubstitted molecules, numerical locations are used to denote positions on the ring. The steps to name polysubsituted rings are as follows:

  1. The principal functional group is defined to be at carbon 1.
  2. In cases where the root can be define using common aromatic names (i.e. toluene) this will often be used.
  3. Substituents are listed alphabetically with numerical locations (bromo before chloro).
  4. Use numbers to give the smallest set of numbers. For example, 4-bromo-2-chloro is better than 3-bromo-5-chloro.

Phenol

Phenol is an aromatic compound containing a hydroxyl group (-OH) bonded directly to the aromatic ring. It is a solid, with a low melting point and is slightly soluble in water.

Note that benzyl alcohol is not an example of a phenol derived molecule. The hydroxy group must be directly bonded to the aromatic ring to be considered phenolic.

Aniline

Aniline is an aromatic compound with an amine group directly attached to the aromatic ring. Aniline is a colourless, oily liquid that is slightly soluble in water. It is a weak base as resonance "spreads" the electron density.

Phenol Resonance Structures

The oxygen lone-pair electrons in neutral phenol can also push electron density into the aromatic ring, although to a lesser degree than in the phenoxide anion. This increases the electron density at the ortho and para positions of the ring, making these carbons more nucleophilic than the meta position. This effect can be seen in the bromination reaction of phenol. Unlike we saw previously with benzene, phenol reacts with bromine rapidly without a catalyst, due to the increased electron density in the ring.

pKa

The pKa of phenol is almost 6 units lower than that of ethanol, meaning that there is ~106 times more phenoxide anion in an aqueous solution of phenol than there is ethoxide ion in a solution of ethanol. This is due to resonance stabilisation of the resulting anion. The negative charge of the phenoxide anion can be delocalised to the ortho and meta positions of the aromatic ring, which stabilises the anion and pushes the equilibrium towards the right.


Aniline

Reactivity

The NH2 group is high activating and ortho- and para- directing.

pKa

As with phenol, the lone pair electrons on the nitrogen of aniline can donate into the aromatic ring. This resonance stabilisation reduces the nucleophilicity of the lone-pair electrons on the nitrogen, which make it a weaker bases. Resonance stabilisation also increases electron density at the ortho and para positions of aniline. The aniline (aminobenzene) aromatic primary amine behaves as a very weak base. Aromatic amines are weaker bases than aliphatic (Not Aromatic) ones due to resonance interaction, which 'spreads' the electron density.

Increased electron density at the ortho and para positions of aniline increase the nucleophilicty of the carbons at these positions.

Bromination

Bromination - Any reaction of process in which bromine (and no other elements) are introduced into a molecule.

The bromination of bezene is an electrophilic aromatic substitution reaction. In this reaction, the electrophile (bromine) forms a sigma bond to the benzene ring, yielding an intermediate. Then, a proton is removed from the intermediate to form a substituted benzene ring. The mechanism for this reaction can be seen in the picture below. Also note that Benzene only reacts with Br2 in the presence of a catalyst.

Now to explain this. Bromines are heckin electronegative, and 'hog the electrons' so to speak in the iron bromide molecule, making the iron in the compound slightly positive (With an oxidation number of three). This causes the iron to act as a Lewis Acid, and accept an electron from somewhere else, specifically from the Br2 molecule, forming a new bond between the iron bromide and the Br2. However in the process of donating this electron, the previously neutral bromine now has a positive charge, and the iron a negative charge.

So nature is basically like the Avatar, and is always striving for balance, which this new molecule is not. Enter Benzene. Now this positive Bromine is hungry for an electron, and will stop at nothing to get one. But where can he get one from? Simple, he can just steal it from the other bromine next to him, making himself complete. But now this Bromine is hungry for electrons (oh no). Instead of snatching back his electron, he instead looks over to his neighbour, Mr Benzene. Now this bromine is so hungry for electrons (Electronegative), he can break apart the aromatic ring and steal one of the electrons from one of the double bonds.

So now we have a Benzene ring with a Bromine attached to one of the carbons, but now the ring's aromaticity is broken and it's very unhappy. To solve it's woes, the other compound present in this intermediate stage, FeBr4- is able to act as a Lewis Base, and steal one of the protons (Hydrogens) from the Benzene ring. In doing this, the Iron compound gives an electron to this Hydrogen (Which is paired to the other Bromine), which is able to replace the electron lost in the inital joining of the Bromine, and restabilises the molecule. Finally, we're left with our original FeBr3, a HBr+ (Which essentially striped the Hydrogen off the Benzene), and a Brominated benzene ring.
