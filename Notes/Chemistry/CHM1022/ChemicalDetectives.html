Calculate a molecular formula from its microanalysis result and determine the number of double bond equivalents from the molecular formula.


Microanalysis

For microanlysis, you'll be given a list of the percentage of each element present in the compound. From this, the Empirical Formula can be determined. So say you're given something like:

  • The following compound was found to have the following mass percents:
    50.8% C, 3.2% H, 8.5% O, 37.5% Cl.
  • Assume 100g of compound, and convert the percentage for each element to the mass in g:
    C = 50.8g, H = 3.2g, O = 8.5g, Cl = 37.5g
  • Determine the number of moles of each element based on 100g. So divide each mass by the elements molar mass:
    C = 50.8/12, H = 3.2/1, O = 8.5/16, Cl = 37.5/36
    C = 4.23, H = 3.2, O = 0.53, Cl = 1.04
  • Determine the molar ratio by dividing each number by the smallest number of moles, and round up to the nearest whole integer (If a ratio value comes up as about half, multiply all the ratio values by two):
    C = 4.23/0.53, H = 3.2/0.53, O = 0.53/0.53, Cl = 1.04/0.53
    C = 8, H = 6, O = 1, Cl = 2
  • Which finally leaves us with the values for our empirical formula:
    C8H6Cl2O

Double Bond Equivalence

Double bond equivalence gives us the degrees of unsaturation, also known as the hydrogen deficiency of the molecule. To determine whether your compound is an aromatic compound and/or contains double bonds, apply the following rule, using the molecular formula CxHyNzOn

  • For a compound with the molecular formula C8H6Cl2O
  • If halogens (F, Cl, Br, I) are present, then y = #Hydrogen Atoms + #Halogen Atoms
    #Rings + Double Bonds = x - (1/2)y + (1/2)z + 1
    DBE = 8 - (1/2) * 8 + (1/2) * 0 + 1
    = 5 Double Bond Equivalents
  • Benzene rings have a Double Bond equivalence of 4, as they have three double bonds, and an extra equivalence due to their delocalised electrons.
    The molecule likely has a Benzene ring, and one other double bond present.

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy!

We use NMR to figure out the number and type of atoms within a molecule and how these atoms are connected to each other. NMR uses a whole lot of physics junk to do with quantum spin and magnets, but I don't think we can be assessed on how it works, so I'm not covering it. Because of the magic that NMR uses, it's really only used two gather data on two elements, 1H and 13C.

They don't really mention 13C NMR at all, so for the sake of time, I'm just going to cover the 1H NMR. In terms of analysis they should be pretty similar, just make sure you check if your spectrum is giving you data on the Carbons or Hydrogens in your molecule.

To standardise reporting of NMR data, chemists have adopted a quantity called chemical shift (δ). This value is expressed as parts per million (ppm) and is derived from the difference between the known magnetic field of the spectrometer and a known standard set at 0 ppm (tetramethylsilane, TMS).

1H NMR

Each signal in a spectrum relates to hydrogens in a specific chemical environment. A hydrogen atom will produce a signal that corresponds to the number of other adjacent hydrogens it can see. In general, the chemical shift of a hydrogen atom can only be influenced another hydrogen atom which is within three σ bonds away and which also sits in a different environment. If there are no hydrogens surrounding it at a distance of three bonds, the hydrogen atom will produce a single peak. If all the hydrogen atoms in a molecule are all chemically equivalent then a single peak will also be produced. For example, benzene gives only one signal in the 1H NMR spectrum because all the hydrogens are chemically equivalent.

To summarise that block of copy and paste: HNMR spectrums have three key features that help to determine a molecules structure.

  1. Chemical Shift and Number of Chemical Enviroments, which gives us information on the functional groups in the molecule, and number of unique hydrogen enviroments.
  2. Integration (Area) Under a Given Signal, which gives us the number of hydrogrens in a given hydrogen enviroment.
  3. Splitting, also known as the most confusing part of HNMR, which tells us about the neighbouring hydrogen enviroments. This is basically the n+1 rule, where a peaks splitting is equal to the number of chemical enviroments neighbouring it, plus one. So if a Hydrogen has one neighbouring hydrogen, it will be a doublet; two neighbours and it will be a triplet, and so on.

Reading and Applying the Spectrum

The NMR spectrum in the image is more accurate/better quality then the one I'm using as a reference. To make sure I don't get anything wrong, I'm using the peaks that are given, rather than actually interpreting that spectrum. The two are pretty similar, so you'll get the gist.

NMR peaks of 7.45 ppm 1H doublet, 7.34 ppm 1H singlet, 7.25 ppm 1H soublet, 2.54 3H singlet are observed.

This indicates that there are four unique hydrogen enviroments, 6 Hydrogens in total (Backing up the Molecular Formula), with one enviroment further away from the others.

The 3H singlet at 2.54 ppm indicates an enviroment with three hydrogens with no neighbouring hydrogens. This means it is not on the aromatic ring, and we can assume it is likely a methyl group. As the other hydrogen enviroments are in close proximity to each other, we can assume they are on the Benzene ring which is further supported by their NMR shifts being in the same region as an aromatic hydrogen, as seen in the table.

Finally making some quick assumptions, we can assume the two doublets are neighbouring hydrogen enviroments (As both are causing each other to split into two peaks), and the remaining hydrogen enviroment is somewhere on the ring where it can't influence these two hydrogen enviroments.


Be a chemical detective, apply the spectroscopy techniques of NMR spectroscopy, IR spectroscopy and Mass Spectroscopy to solve chemical structures.


Using the same example that has been used in each of the previous sections.

Microanalysis

Microanalysis is used to determine the Empirical Formula of an unknown compound. This is the first, and probably most important step of solving chemical structures, because if you get this wrong, your entire molecule will be wrong. Refer to the Microanalysis section further up for how to do this.

The microanalysis determined that the Empirical Formula of the compound is C8H6Cl2O.

Mass Spectroscopy

When you look at the graph, you're looking for the parent ion which is usually the highest peak (Be aware that some isotopes can play shinanigans and produce peaks at a higher m/z value than the parent ion). Get the m/z value for the highest peak, and you've got the (approximate) molar mass of your compound! Here we can see that the peak is around 188 - 189 m/z, and thus the molar mass of our compound is 188 - 189.

We use this data to determine the Molecular Formula of our compound. To do this, you add up the molar mass of your Emperical Formula, which for our molecule is 189, and compare this with the parent ion from the Mass Spectra. If they're equal (Or close to it), then your Molecular Formula is the same as your Empirical Formula, and you can move on. For the scope of this course, if your molar mass is different to the parent ion, you divide or multiply your empirical formula until you get the sameish value.

Double Bond Equivalence

This is used to determine the number of double bonds and/or aromatic rings in the compound. Usually if this is equal to four or more, you have an aromatic ring (Just because they wouldn't give you a molecule with so many double bonds). Refer to the Double Bond Equivalence section above for how to do this.

DBE = 8 - (1/2) * 8 + (1/2) * 0 + 1 = 5 Double Bond Equivalents.

Therefore this molecule likely has a Benzene ring and one other double bond.

Infrared Spectroscopy

IR Spectroscopy is used to determine the presence of functional groups in the compound. Again, for how to do this, see the IR Spectroscopy section above.

So the 3100 cm-1 indicates the molecule is aromatic, the 2600 cm-1 indicates an alkane and the 1700 cm-1 indicates a C=O stretch of some sort.

Determining the Chemical Structure

So what do we know? We know that the final structure should have 8 Carbons, 6 Hydrogens, 2 Chlorines and 1 Oxygen, thanks to the microanalysis/mass spectroscopy. We also know it should have 5 double bond equivalents, and that there will probably be a Benzene ring to satisfy 4 of them. We know that there will be an alkane enviroment somewhere, a C=O stretch somewhere and an aromatic ring from the IR Spectroscopy. And finally we know that there will be 4 Hydrogen enviroments, one with three Hydrogens (Likely a Methyl group) which has no neighbouring Hydrogens, two neighbouring each other with one hydrogen each on the Benzene ring, and one Hydrogen on the benzene ring, neighbouring no other hydrogens. Putting this all together we're left with:

2',4'-dichloroacetophenone

Really after you get all your data, just play around with the structures until you get one that works.
