Explain chemical attributes of Group 6 elements.


Group 6 Elements

d-block metals have clear trends that you need to be familiar with. All d-block elements are metals, they all form compounds in a variety of oxidation states, form coloured compounds and exhibit magnetic properties.

Taken from Moodle, which in turn was taken from Blackman et al. Chemistry, 3rd Edition

From this table you can see that there is an increase in atomic radius down the group from Cr to Mo, but the radii of Mo and W are the same. This is due to Lanthanide Contraction, which refers to the fact that the 5s and 5p orbitals penetrate the 4f sub-shell so the 4f orbital is not shielded from the increasing nuclear change, which causes the atomic radius of the atom to decrease. This decrease in size continues throughout the series. The result of this contraction is that the properties of the first element in the group, Cr, differ from those of the 2nd and 3rd members of the group, Mo and W.

Ionisation Energy

The first ionisation energy increase down the group is due to the combination of an increase in the effective nuclear charge with a similar metallic radius. However subsequent removal of electrons from the 2nd and 3rd row transition metals becomes easier due to shielding by the innermost electrons. This leads to a much more gradual increase in the second and subsequent ionisation energies.

Consequently the 2nd and 3rd row elements become more stable in higher oxidation states and are much more easily oxidised in low oxidation states (+2 and +3) than the corresponding first-row transition metals.

To Summarise: Ionisation Energy, or the energy it takes to remove one electron from an element, increases as the proton number increases and the atomic radius decreases (From the more postively charged nucleus having a greater pull on the electrons). So as you go down a group, the first ionisation energy increases. However in group 6 elements, shielding by the innermost electrons makes it easier to remove electrons from outer layers, which makes the jump in first ionisation energies down the group not as large as you'd expecet.

Oxoanions

Oxoanions are negatively charged ions containing two or more elements, one of which is oxygen. Group 6 elements commonly form oxoanions, where the negatively charged ion is formed from oxygen and either Cr, Mn, or W.

I assume they'll give the formula of the oxoanion that you need to determine the state and structure of, so really just remember that the oxidation state of oxygen is -2 usually, and solve for the unknown metal.

Describe (or Discuss) the use of Cr compounds as oxidising and reducing agents.


Chromium

Chromium has the ground state configuration 4s1 3d5. You should apparently note the influence of a stable half-filled shell of electrons. The common oxidation states of chromium are +2, +3 and +6.

Chromium Compounds

Chromium can produce a heckton of different oxoanions depending on it's oxidation state and the other elements present. For example, H2CrO4 acts as an acid as it reacts with OH- anions to produce a yellow coloured CrO42- and water through a condensation reaction. CrO42- is then able to react with H+ in acidic conditions to produce the orange coloured dichromate Cr2O72- and again, water. Apparently you should note how the briding oxygen atom links the two Cr6+ ions.

The dichromate Cr2O72-, which has an oxidation state Cr6+, acts as a strong oxidiser (itself gets reduced) in acid to from Cr3+, which is the most stable oxidation state of Chromium.

Chromium can also exist in low oxidation states, Cr0 and Cr2+. They are strong reducing agents and easily oxidise to Cr3+. As the oxidation state of chromium increases (2+ to 3+ to 6+), its tendency to remove more elctrons decreases (ionisation energy increases) making it more electronegative, less metallic and more acidic (More able to accept an electron from a donor compound, ie Lewis Acid). Note that Cr2O3 is amphoteric, which means it can behave as an acid or base.

Summary of Chromium Oxides

Okay, real quick summary of that crap: Chromium can exist in multiple oxidation states, namely +0, +2, +3 and +6. The oxidation state +3 is the most stable, and chromiums in other oxidation states will act as either strong reducing agents (+0 and +2) or oxidisising agents(+6) to get back to that +3 oxidation state. As the oxidation state of chromium increasesm, it's ionisation energy increases, it becomes less metallic and more acidic.


Identify characteristics of coordination complexes and coordination compounds.


Coordination Complexes

A Coordination Complex consists of a central cation (Metal Ion) bonded to molecules or anions (called ligands).

Coordination complexes form because the positive metal ion is a Lewis Acid (They accept electron pairs), and the ligands surrounding it are Lewis Bases (They donate electron pairs). The bond formed is known as a coordinate bond, a donor covalent bond or a dative bond.

Any ion or molecule with a pair of nonbonding electrons can be a ligand. Many ligands are described as monodentate (literally, "one-toothed") because they "bite" the metal in only one place. Typical monodentate ligands are given in the figure below.

Transition-metal ions form coordination complexes because they have empty valence-shell orbitals that can accept pairs of electrons from a Lewis base.

Coordination Compounds

A Coordination Compound is a salt (Cation + Anion) in which one or both of the ions is a coordination complex. Counter Ions may be present to maintain charge balance in a coordination compound. A compound may dissociate into anions and cations in solution (Behaving as an electrolyte), and ligands remain attached to the metal ion. These ionic solutions can also conduct electricity.

Determining Metal Oxidation State in Coordination Complex

  • Determine the charge of the complex. For example: Nitrate (counter ion) has a charge of -1 so complex must have +1 charge.
  • Determine the charges of the ligands. For example: Cl- is -1 and NH3 is neutral.
  • Oxidation state of metal = Complex charge – sum of ligand charges. Here, oxidation state of metal = +1 – (2 x -1) = +3.

Describe the geometries of coordination complexes.


Geometries of Coordination Complexes

We got Kepert Theory to help us here: Complex shapes depend on repulsion of ligands. We've also got some sweet new terms to define:

Coordination Number is the number of donor atoms around metal.

Donor Atoms are the atom that forms the coordination bond with the metal. eg. For NH3 ligand, the nitrogen forms the bond with the metal, thus nitrogen is the donor atom.

The Geometry of a Coordination Complex depends on the Coordination number and the nature of the ligands.

  • Linear – 2 donor atoms.
  • Trigonal Planar – 3 donor atoms.
  • Tetrahedral – 4 donor atoms (Exception for some 4-coordinate complexes which are square planar).
  • Trigonal Bipyramidal or square-based pyramidal - 5 donor atoms.
  • Octahedral - 6 donor atoms.
