Distinguish the 4 classes of hydrocarbon.


Alkanes

Saturated hydrocarbons consisting of only carbon-carbon single bonds. Have an -ane suffix

Alkenes

Unsaturated hydrocarbons containing one or more carbon-carbon double bonds. Have an -ene suffix.

Alkenes have the general formula C­nH2n and contain two sp2 hybridised carbon atoms which form the double bond, made of one sigma-bond, and one pi-bond. By considering either hybridisation or VSEPR, the shape of an alkene is predicted to be such that both carbons attached to that double bond are trigonal planar.

Alkynes

Unsaturated hydrocarbons containing one or more carbon-carbon triple bonds. Have an -yne suffix.

Similarly to alkenes, alkynes are made with two sp-hybridised orbitals, which bind to give one sigma-bond, and two pi-bonds. Both hybridisation and VSEPR predict a linear geometry with both carbons attached to that triple bond.

Arenes

Contains one or more benzene like rings.

Understand how structure impacts properties of simple alkanes.


Alkane Structure and Properties

Alkanes are made entirely of sp3 hybridised carbons, with the simplest member being methane. Due to this hybridisation a methane molecule has a tetrahedral geometry with all C-H bonds at 109.5 degrees from each other.

While methane, the simplest hydrocarbon can only have one shape, alkanes with two or more carbons can have various three dimensional structures called conformations. They result not by chainging the bond angles (Which are all fixed at 109.5°, but by rotating around sigma-bonds.

Staggered Conformation is where the roation of the sigma-bond creates the longest distance between the atoms attached to each carbon.

Eclipsed Conformation is when the atoms on each carbon are as close as possible to each other.

As we increase the number of carbon & hydrogen atoms on the hydrocarbon molecule, the properties of the molecule changes, with melting point, boiling point and density all increasing. This is a result of increased intermolecular horses between the molecules.

Drawing 3D structures

Most molecules have bonds on the x-, y- and z-axes, which we need to represent on paper somehow. How do?

For the purposes of this course, we use Dashed and Wedged Bonds to represent 3D structure. With this convention, regular lines represent bonds on the 2D plane, Dashed bonds are pointing away from you, and Wedged bonds are pointing towards you.

So if the line is a completely solid wedge, then since wedges are delicious, it's coming towards you. If the line is a not solid series of dashes, then much like how Dashing people run away from you, those bonds are going away from you.

This method of drawing cyclohexane molecules is used to more accurately display the bond angles of 109.5 degrees between carbon atoms.

Chair Conformation

The chair conformation is the lowest energy conformation and the most common. In this, there are two planes with carbon atoms alternating the plane that they are in, making carbon atoms 1, 3 and 5 in one plane and carbon atoms 2, 4 and 6 in another plane (Starting with the top right corner being carbon 1). There are two possible positions for hydrogen atoms (or other substituents) to be located: axial and equatorial. The axial position is not parallel to any of the carbon-carbon bonds while the equatorial position is parallel to one or more of the carbon-carbon bonds.

The carbon atoms 1, 3 and 5 have the axial positions pointing up while equatorial is pointing down; while carbon atoms 2, 4 and 6 have the axial position as the carbon pointing down, while the equatorial pointing up. This creates very little strain between the molecules, due to the alternating bonds, making it the most stable.

Boat Conformation

The boat conformation has the highest energy of all three conformations. In the boat conformation carbon atoms 2, 3, 5, and 6 are in the same plane with carbon atoms 1 and 4 in another plane. The boat conformation is very high energy due to several factors, includign the flagpole effect, where the two equatorial bonds of carbon 1 and carbon 4 end up pointing towards each other, making them very close and interfering with each other causing steric tension.

This coformation is unstable due to this strain.



Assign stereochemistry of alkenes.


Alkene Stereochemistry

As pi-bonds cannot rotate, this allows alkenes to have isomers called diastereoisomers. As opposed to constitutional isomers, in which the conectivity of the atoms is different (and which alkanes can have as well), diastereoisomers have the same connectivity but different orientation in space. To figure out if two molecules are diastereoisomers, follow the checklist below:

  • ✓ Are they isomers? Is the formula identical?
  • ✖ Are they constitutional isomers. Is the connectivity different?
  • ✖ Are they the same molecule. Rotate / Redraw the molecule.
  • ✖ Are they mirror images of each other.

As for naming, if the higher priority groups (Greater the atomic number, greater the priority) are on the same side, we call the molecule cis. If they are are on opposite sites, we call the molecule trans.

Alkene Reaction Mechanisms

Breaking an alkane C-C sigma-bond requires 348 kJ mol-1. An alkene is 612 kJ mol-1, because two bonds are broken, the sigma-bond worth 348 kJ mol-1 and the pi-bond worth 264 kJ mol-1. Reactions of alkenes that we will discuss don’t break both bonds but rather break the weakest bond, the pi-bond to give new products.

Define - Carbocation - A chemical species which contains a positively charged carbon atom with only three bonds to it.

Alkenes have two pairs of electrons (Found in the sigma bond and the pi bond) between two carbon atoms, and so we consider them to be electron rich. As they are electron rich they are attracted to positive charges, such as nuclei, so we call them nucleophiles. Nucleophiles react with things that like electrons, called Electrophiles.

Hydrobromination - Mechanism of HBr reacting with an Alkene

Equation of Reaction

The reaction of Hydrobromic Acid and Alkene occurs in two steps:

  • Bromination of Alkene Reaction Mechanism
  • Step 1
  • In the first step a pair of electrons move from the pi-bond of the nucleophilic alkene to the electrophilic atom of H-Br (note that H-Br has a permanent dipole due to the high electronegativity of bromine). This breaks the H-Br bond producing a molecule called "cyclohexyl carbocation" and bromide anion. (Note that the arrows indicate movement of electrons).
  • Step 2
  • The bromide anion acts as the nucleophile and adds to the electrophilic carbocation formed from the previous step to give the observed product.

Energetics of Reaction

Breaking bonds costs energy, while making releases energy. So looking at the reaction we can see that in the first step, the pi-bond in the alkene is broken, the sigma-bond in the HBr is broken, and an ionic interaction formed. In the second step no bonds are broken, buy a sigma-bond is gained. Knowing that breaking bonds costs energy, and the first step of the reaction breaks two of them and only forms one (as opposed to the second reaction which breaks two), we would expect the activaiton energy of the first step is larger than the second. As a consequence the step that defines the overall rate of this reaction (as in the slower of the two steps), known as the rate determining step, is the first step. Markovnikov's Rule

While the reaction described above only produced a single product, this is not always the case. If a non-symmetrical alkene is reacted with HBr, there are two prodcuts that can be produced. These are constitutional isomers with the position of the hydrogen and bromine switched. So the question is, which one will form? Well basically, if you look at the energy diagram for the reaction, the pathway that forms a more stable carbocation will form more quickly, and thus be the major product. Our boy Markovnikov proposed the following rule for non-symmetrical alkene addition reactions with H-X (Where X is an electronegative element).

"The alkene carbon that has the most hydrogens begins to gain more."

So following this rule, the hydrogen from HBr will bond with the alkene carbon with the larger number of hydrogens.

AHHH Side-note: the prefix "regio-" indicates a preference of one over another. Therefore a "regioisomer" is an isomer which its structure is preferential over other isomers. "Regioselective" is where one direction of bond breaking or bond forming occurs in preference to all others.

Carbocation Stability

While we could use Markovnikov's nice and easy rule to predict the outcome of a non-symmetrical alkene, you could also arrive at the same conclusion by considering the stability of the carbocations involved if you really wanted to. At a fundemental level, the more charge can be spread out, the more stable the cation will be.

Two main factors determine how spread out the charge is:

  • Inductive Effects
  • A positively charged atom attracts electrons from adjacent sigma-bonds. As such the charge is spread between these atoms. So the more sigma-bonds, the more stabilised the carbocation is. Hence, the following order of carbocation stability arises.
