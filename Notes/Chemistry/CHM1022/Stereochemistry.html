Define the following terms: isomer, stereoisomerism, enantiomer, diastereoisomer, chiral, achiral, stereogenic centre, optical activity, racemic mixture.


  • notesIsomer

    Isomers are molecules which possess the same molecular formula but differ in structural connectivity, and thus have different properties.

  • notesStereoisomerism

    Stereoisomers are isomeric molecules that have the same molecular formula and sequence of bonded atoms, but differ in the three-dimensional orientations of their atoms in space.

    In general, a molecule with n stereocentres with have a maximum of 2n stereoisomers.

  • notesEnantiomer

    An enantiomer is one of the two molecules that are mirror images of each other and are non-superimposable.

  • notesDiastereoisomer

    Diastereoisomers are stereoisomers which are not enantiomers (not mirror images of each other).

  • notesChiral

    Molecules that are non-superimposable mirror images of one another are said to be chiral.

    In order for a tetrahedral centre to be chiral it must have four different substituents attached to it and thus have no plane of symmetry.

  • notesAchiral

    A compound or object which is superimposable on its mirror image is referred to as achiral. Achiral molecules have a plane of symmetry.

    In order for a carbon to be achiral, the carbon must bear two identical substituents in order for the mirror image of itself to be superimposable.

  • notesStereogenic Centre

    The most common type of enantiomerism in organic molecules is the tetrahedral carbon atom with four different substituents. This centre can also be referred to as an asymmetric carbon centre or as a stereogenic centre.

  • notesOptical Activity

    The only physical property in which enantiomers differ is how they interact differently with plane-polarized light. The plane of plane-polarised light is rotated which light passes through a solution of the enantiomer. This property of a chiral compound is called optical activity. The rotation is called optical rotation.

  • notesRacemic Mixture

    A mixture of equal amounts of two enantiomers.

    Racemic Mixtures are optically inactive, as they rotate plane-polarised light in exactly equal amounts in oppoiste directions.


Understand the function of a polarimeter.


Polarimeter

"An instrument for measuring the polarization of light, and especially for determining the effect of a substance in rotating the plane of polarization of light."

Imagine you're walking down the street, when suddenly you find an otherwise sterile beaker containing a mysterious white powder. As an upstanding citizen, you pick it up to dispose of it in the local chemical waste bucket, but just before you arrive a studious terrorist comes up to you and demands to know the optical activity of the powder you're currently holding. What do?

In this scenario, you'd pull out your trusty polarimeter, a device used to measure the rotation of plane-polarised light. Once you make a solution of your powder with your flask of deionized water you keep on you, and put it in the machine, you're going to need to interpret the results.

First you need to ask, what's happening? What even is plane-polarised light? This is actually quite tricky, and strays dangerously into physics territory, but basically, light travels as a wave osicilating randomly in all directions which is what we'd call unpolarised. Plane-polarised light however, only oscilates in a single plane perpendicular to the direction of ordinary light.

When light passes through a solution containing a chiral molecule, the light is able to interact with the molecules electron cloud. When plane-polarised light passes through this, the direction of the light changes, and thus rotates the plane in which it is oscilating. Two enantiomers will rotate plane-polarised light by the same magnitude in opposite directions, while a racemic solution or achiral molecule will not rotate plane-polarised light. So by measuring the rotation of light that bounces off an enantiomer, we can determine the optical activity of a molecule.

Now that we know what's happening, we need to interpret the results. When a beam of plane-polarised light is rotated counterclockwise the molecule is in the (0,L) form. When a beam of plane-polairsed light is rotated clockwise the molecule is of the (+, D) form. Make sure not to confuse this with the R/S notation, as they indicate the arrangement of groups at the stereogenic centre, while the polarimeter is indicating the optical activity. These two notations are independent of each other.

The terrorist, satisified with your analysis of the powder decides to spare your life and walks off into the sunset, leaving you baffled and confused.


Draw stereo representations for simple organic compounds.


Frick on a stick this is hard to explain in words.

We represent chemical structures with lines representing bonds, however, this representation gives no information about stereochemistry associated with the molecule (ie. It doesn't represent the spatial arrangement of bonds).

In order to represent spatial arrangements of bonds, chemists have come up with the system which utilises wedge-dash notation in order to represent molecules as being three-dimensional. In this notation, two bonds are drawn as sticks to represent them being in the plane of the page, one bond is drawn as a solid wedge to represent the bond being projected towards the viewer and one dashed bond to represent the bond being projected away from the viewer.

So you could remember it by: You love potato wedges, so you want them coming towards you. Of course, all these wedges have made you fat, so dashing people run away from you.


Use the Cahn-Ingold-Prelog rules to assign configuration at a stereogenic centre.


Assigning Configurations to Stereogenic Centres

A chiral centre can have two possible configurations R and S. The letter R and S are derived from the Latin words for right and left, 'rectus' and 'sinister'. In order to assign a stereogenic centre as either R or S you need to follow the following three steps:

  1. Locate the stereogenic centre and identify the four different substituents.
  2. Assign priorities (1 to 4) to all substituents attached to the chiral centre, using the following steps:
    1. Assign priorities of substituents according to the Cahn-Ingold-Prelog prioritisation scheme which gives the highest priority to the atom with the highest atomic number. H, C, N, O, F, S, Cl, Br, I (increasing priority) (larger atomic number = higher priority).
    2. If the two atoms or substituents are the same (for example if there are two CH2 groups attached to the stereogenic carbon), proceed to the next position of the chain. You may need to go even further until the first point of difference.
    3. Groups with Π-bonds are given single-bond equivalents by duplication or triplication of multiply bonded atoms.
  3. Rotate the group or atom with the lowest priority (priority 4) so it is orientated away from you. Next, draw a curve from the first-priority substituent through to the second, and then the third substituent. Determine if the direction of this priority curve is clockwise (R) or anticlockwise (S).

Demonstrate an understanding of the significance of chirality in biology and medicine, by giving an example.


Chirality in Biology

This one's pretty simple. A heck ton of molecules in nature exhibit chirality with a certain "handedness" (This probably wont be assessed, but most amino acids are 'left-handed', and most sugars are 'right-handed'), especially for receptors and enzymes in organisms. Because most medicine is basically throwing random chemicals at humans and hoping they positively interact with some receptor or enzyme in the body, chirality is pretty important, since a racemic mixture is basically two drugs in one solution.

The famous, and likely assessed example of chirality causing problems in medicine is thalidomide, a drug introduced in 1957 to help pregnant women with morning sickness among other ailments. But dun dun dun, thalidomide had one chiral carbon, and the mixture sold was a racemic mixture of the R and S enantiomers. While the R enantiomer provided the theraputic benefit of the drug, the S enantiomer was much more sinister. As enantiomers react differently towards other chiral molecules (Such as proteins), the S enantiomer was teratogenic, and caused thousands of birth defects.

Alcohol Substitutions

Haloalkanes

Just as alcohols are useful starting points for organic chemists to make other new compounds, so are haloalkanes.

Haloalkanes are a class of compounds containing a halogen (X) atom covalently bonded to an sp3 hybridised carbon atom. The C-X bond in haloalkanes is polarized due to the electronegativity difference between the carbon and the halogen. This makes the carbon bearing the halogen partially positively polarized, hence able to attract an electron rich and/or negatively charged species.

Conversion of the alcohol to the alkyl halide involves substitution of halogen for -OH. The most common reagents for this purpose are the halogen acids, HX, and thionyl chloride, (SOCl2). The halogen acids include HCl, HBr and HI.

Water-soluble tertiary alcohols react very rapidly with HCl, HBr and HI. Low-molar-mass, water-soluble primary and secondary alcohols do not react under these conditions. 1° and 2° alcohols are converted to bromoalkanes and iodoalkanes by treatment with concentrated HBr and HI.
