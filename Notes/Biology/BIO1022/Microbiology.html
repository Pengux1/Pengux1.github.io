Learning Objectives

  • Describe the major domains of unicellular organisms.
  • Describe how prokaryotes differ from eukaryotes.
  • Describe the appearance of Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria as seen in the visiblelight microscope.
  • Explain the basis of the two different responses of bacteria to the Gram stain.
  • Define the features that separate Archaea from bacteria.
  • List the three major groups of Archaea.
  • List some major groups of Gram positive and Gram negative bacteria.
  • Describe three common shapes of bacteria.
  • Describe host sites where microbiota are normally found, and where they are not.
  • Describe the main properties that enable pathogens to damage the host.
  • Distinguish between parasite, pathogen and normal flora (microbiota).
  • Distinguish between ‘infection’ and ‘disease’ and explain how the microbiota provide a host with some protection against pathogenic bacteria.

A process that allows bacteria to attach or adhere to other cells and surfaces. Adhesion is an important step for colonization of a new host or environment and can contribute to bacterial pathogenesis.

A domain of single-celled microorganisms. These microbes are prokaryotes, meaning they have no cell nucleus.

A rod-shaped bacterium.

The division of a single entity into two or more parts and the regeneration of those parts into separate entities resembling the original.

A very large structure of many bacteria. It is a polysaccharide layer that lies outside the cell envelope, and is thus deemed part of the outer envelope of a bacterial cell. It is a well-organized layer, not easily washed off, and it can be the cause of various diseases.

Any bacterium or archaeon that has a spherical, ovoid, or generally round shape. It is one of the three distinct bacterial shapes, the other two being bacillus (rod-shaped) and spiral-shaped cells.

When bacteria are in or on the body, but not causing illness.

The biofilm found naturally on teeth

Also known as tooth decay, an epidemic, microbiological contagious disease of the teeth that ends in localized dissolution and damage of the calcified structure of the teeth.

A dormant, tough, and non-reproductive structure produced by certain bacteria from the Firmicute phylum. It is a stripped-down, dormant form to which the bacterium can reduce itself.

Eukaryotic cells are defined by the presence of a nucleus surrounded by a complex nuclear membrane. Also, eukaryotic cells are characterized by the presence of membrane-bound organelles in the cytoplasm. Organelles such as mitochondria, the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), Golgi apparatus, lysosomes, and peroxisomes are held in place by the cytoskeleton, an internal network that supports transport of intracellular components and helps maintain cell shape (Figure 1). The genome of eukaryotic cells is packaged in multiple, rod-shaped chromosomes as opposed to the single, circular-shaped chromosome that characterizes most prokaryotic cells.

An appendage that can be found on many Gram-negative and some Gram-positive bacteria that are used by bacteria to adhere to one another and to adhere to animal cells and some inanimate objects.

Bacteria that do not retain the crystal violet stain used in the gram-staining method of bacterial differentiation. Gram negative cell walls contain a thin peptidoglycan layer (without teichoic acids) that is surrounded by a thick plasma membrane.

Bacteria that give a positive result in the Gram stain test. Gram positive cell walls contain a thick peptidoglycan layer with teichoic acids. Gram positive bacteria will stain purple because of their thick peptidoglycan cell wall.

An "ecological community of commensal, symbiotic and pathogenic microorganisms" found in and on all multicellular organisms studied to date from plants to animals.

An infection caused by pathogens (bacteria, viruses, fungi, or protozoa) that take advantage of an opportunity not normally available, such as a host with a weakened immune system, an altered microbiota (such as a disrupted gut microbiota), or breached integumentary barriers.

A hair-like appendage found on the surface of many bacteria. The terms pilus and fimbria (Latin for 'fringe'; plural: fimbriae) can be used interchangeably, although some researchers reserve the term pilus for the appendage required for bacterial conjugation.

A unicellular organism that lacks a membrane-bound nucleus, mitochondria, or any other membrane-bound organelle.

The ability of a drug to targets sites that are relative specific to the microorganism responsible for infection. Sometimes these sites are unique to the microorganism or simply more essential to survival of the microorganism than to the host.
