Describe the different theories of motivation.


Motivation - The wants or needs that direct behaviour toward a goal. Three components

Activation - it activates or energises behaviour

Direction - It gives direction or purpose to behaviour

Persistence - It gives us to persist?

Motive - Something that brings together all the different reasons for a behvaiour

Motivation is influenced by: physiological factors, emotional factors (Thoughts, expectations that you will succeed), cognitive factors, social factors (Influenced by peers, family friends, what is seen on tv). These factors may simultaneously influence your motivation.

Theories of Motivation

Instinct Theory

An instinct is an automatic, involuntary, and unlearned behaviour that is consistently triggered by a particular stimuli.

Evolutionary Approach - Where tendencies evolved as they helped with enviromental adaption.

Drive Reduction Theory

- Centred around the principle of homeostasis – the tendency for organisms to keep physiological systems at a steady level:

Homeostasis - For particular orggnisms there is an ideal state for them to be operating at.

- An imbalance in homeostasis generates a biological need and the organism experiences a feeling of arousal or a drive, to take action and restore balance.

Physiological needs create drives. Primary drives are biological requirements such as food or water; Secondary Drives are learned requirements such as $$$CashMonay$$$, recognition, status.

Arousal Theory

Arousal – level of physiological activation as measured by brain function, heart rate or muscle tension

Yerkes-Dodson Law states that for most people the optimal level of arousal is when it is moderate.

Moderate levels of arousal lead to optimal performance.

-Think of your arousal level regarding taking an exam for this class. If your level is very low, such as boredom and apathy, your performance will likely suffer. Similarly, a very high level, such as extreme anxiety, can be paralyzing and hinder performance.

We are motivated to behave in ways that maintain one’s optimal level of arousal - optimal level of arousal differs between people e.g., an athlete may have a low optimal arousal level and performs best when arousal is low, while another athlete may perform best when arousal level is high

Incentive Theory

The “carrot and stick” approach to motivation – offering rewards to motivate people and elicit desired behaviour from them (i.e., the carrot) and discouraging other behaviours (i.e., the stick).

Carrot - reward, stick - punishment. It means that this model provides both being motivated to get reward, and motivated to avoid punishment.

Enviromental stimuli motivates behaviour: Motivated to get positive incentives (rewards) i.e. bonus, promotions. Motivated to avoid negative incentives (punishments). i.e. Losing your job.

The Candle Problem

Give someone a candle, some thumbtacks and matches and ask them how to attach it to the wall so the wax doesn't drip on the floor.

It took 5 to 10 minutes for most people. When thumbtacks were taken out of box, usually solved fairly fast.

The group with an incentive only performed better when the thumbtacks were out of thebox at the beginning.

Rewards can narrow our focus. Extrinsic motivation provides external rewards, which narrow the. Rewards for problems that do not have a clear solution often don't work.



Understand the control of hunger and eating.


Maslow's Hierachy of Needs

According to this model of motivation, our main primal motivations are the need to drink water, eat food and procreate – because they are necessary for our survival.

These are our physiological needs and until met we do not tend to focus on other needs that are higher up the pyramid e.g., people who are extremely hungry are highly motivated to find food - becoming obsessed over where it will come from and how they will obtain it.

Minnesota Starvation Experiment

For three months the calroies they were given is halved. Enter starvation state, their thoughts and emotions were recorded.

Food became sole source of motivation and fascination. Began obsessively thinking about food. Other needs were being replaced by thoughts of food.

Eating Disorders

Obesity

Characterised by a body mass index greater than 30.

Reduces life span by an average of 6 to 7 years.

A growing problem with children. Becoming more of a problem over time. Availabilty of fast food, influence of parents.

Some causes - Genetic predisposition, living a sedentry life - employees spend longer sitting at work

Caused by inadequate physical activity combined with overeating.

More than 60% of Australian adults are obese (and almost 10% are severely obese)!

Treatments include:

Bariatric Surgery (Lapband Surgery)

Medication

Weight Loss Programs

More of a health condition and related to the idea of being motivated to eat, and the causes - what motivates someone to eat more than another?

Anorexia Nervosa

Eating disorders - When the eating cycle is disrupted for some reason.

Characterised by behaviours resulting in weight loss below 85% of normal.

About 95% of sufferers are young females.

Causes serious, often irreversible physical damage.

Incidence appears to be increasing.

Causes are likely a combination of genetic, biochemical, social and physiological factors.

Treatments include:

Drugs

Hospitalisation

Pyschotherapy

Bulimia

Intense fear of being fat, but may be any weight.

Estimates range from 1-3% in young women.

Binge-purge episodes up to twice per day.

Most with bulimia see their eating habits as problematic.

Associated with wanting to be in control. Stages of binging and purging.

Usually not life-threatening.

Consequences include:

Dehydration and nutritional problems

Dental problems due to acids from vomiting.

Treatments include:

Individual or group therapy.

Antidepressant drugs.
